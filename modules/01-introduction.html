Module 01

Introduction to Site Reliability Engineering

Understand what SRE is, why it exists, and the mindset shift from reactive ops to engineering reliability.

What is SRE?

Site Reliability Engineering (SRE) is what happens when you treat operations as a software problem. Instead of manually keeping systems running, SRE teams use software engineering practices — automation, monitoring, and rigorous design — to build and maintain large-scale, reliable services.

The discipline was pioneered at Google and codified in the Site Reliability Engineering book. Today, SRE practices are used across cloud-native companies of every size.

SRE vs. Traditional Operations

  • Proactive vs. reactive — SREs design for failure before it happens.
  • Engineering vs. ticket-driven — At least 50% of SRE time should go to engineering work, not manual toil.
  • Data-driven — Reliability decisions are backed by SLIs and SLOs, not gut feel.
  • Shared ownership — Development and operations collaborate on reliability, not throw problems over a wall.
Key principle SRE is not a job title — it's a set of principles and practices that any engineering team can adopt.

The SRE Mindset

  1. Embrace risk — 100% uptime is impossible and expensive. Define how much unreliability is acceptable.
  2. Automate everything repetitive — If you do it more than twice, script it.
  3. Measure what users feel — Internal metrics matter less than user-facing reliability.
  4. Learn from failure — Every incident is a chance to make the system more resilient.

Core SRE Practices

  • Service Level Objectives (SLOs) and error budgets
  • On-call rotations with clear escalation paths
  • Blameless postmortems after incidents
  • Capacity planning and load testing
  • Progressive rollouts and feature flags
  • Runbooks and automated remediation

Who Should Learn SRE?

This course is designed for software engineers, DevOps practitioners, platform engineers, and anyone responsible for keeping production systems healthy. Basic familiarity with Linux, networking, and at least one programming language is helpful but not required.
